Frequently Asked Questions

What is this?

»WISP« is a simple password prompter.

Why should I use one?

Common passwords are not safe. All passwords that contain words which can be found in a dictionary are vulnerable to attack. The same applies to passwords that are to short. Take a look at this article to find out how long your password would stand against brute force attacks. Also take into account that computing power doubles every two years.
However, people choose these passwords because they are easy to remember, putting themselves and others at risk.
»WISP« lets you choose an "easy to remember"-password and gives you a "hard to guess"-password in return.

How does it work?

When a user enters a »weak password« that hasn't been used before, two »strong passwords« are generated at random.
Once created, the passwords are stored in a database and are linked to that specific »weak password« from now on. This means, you can always come back and retrieve your »strong passwords« with the appropriate »weak password«.

Is it safe?

You might find it confusing that the passwords are available to everyone, but out of context they usually serve no purpose.
However, you should not use a too easily guessable »weak password«, like "password" or any of the other among the 500 most common passwords.
Using "facebook" for your Facebook account isn't smart either.
The data itself is transmitted with 128-bit encryption via TLS 1.0. The app runs on Google Appengine servers, which means that it is accessible at all times and even through major catastrophes.

Why are there so many passwords?

Another problem that comes for the same reasons is password reuse. In order to mitigate this effect, two »strong passwords« are given: One for sites you trust and another for those you're not sure about. They are both linked to the same »weak password«.
